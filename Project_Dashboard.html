Research Overview

Problem Statement

People in India often code-mix Hindi and Marathi on digital platforms, usually typed in Roman script. Marathi is a low-resource language, with very limited research in NLP. In Maharashtra (India), many users are bilingual and prefer Roman script for online chats.

Research Gap:

No dedicated solutions exist for Hindi–Marathi code-mixing, leaving a major research gap in NLP.

Key Challenges

  • Irregular spellings & phonetic variations (e.g., bahut / bohot / bahot)
  • Ambiguity in words (kaal = "yesterday" in Marathi, "bad time" in Hindi)
  • Noisy user-generated text – informal grammar, hashtags, mentions, abbreviations
  • Low-resource language – Marathi lacks large annotated datasets
  • Transliteration errors – Roman → Devanagari conversion introduces diacritic mistakes and inconsistent mappings

Preprocessing Pipeline

Roman Script Preprocessing

  • Converted all text to lowercase for consistency
  • Removed noise: URLs, user mentions, hashtags
  • Stopword removal using NLTK English list
  • Tokenization of text into words
  • Lemmatization to reduce words to base form

Roman → Devanagari Transliteration

Used Indic-Transliteration (sanscript module) for automatic conversion with custom word mappings to reduce diacritic and spelling errors:

word_mappings = {
    # Hindi common words
    "hai": "है", "hota": "होता", "mujhe": "मुझे", "tumhara": "तुम्हारा",

    # Marathi common words
    "ahe": "आहे", "mala": "मला", "tula": "तुला",

    # Common expressions
    "namaste": "नमस्ते", "namaskar": "नमस्कार",
    "dhanyavad": "धन्यवाद", "shukriya": "शुक्रिया"
}

Example transformation:

"tumhara dost mala madat karta hai"

→ "तुम्हारा दोस्त मला मदत करता है"

Devanagari Preprocessing

  • Removed punctuation and normalized whitespace
  • Stopword removal for Hindi & Marathi using tailored lists:
hindi_stopwords = ["और", "का", "की", "के", ...]
marathi_stopwords = ["आणि", "आहे", "आहेत", "होते", ...]

Model Accuracy Comparison: Baseline vs Fine-Tuned (K-Fold)

Key Observations

  • MuRIL showed the most significant improvement after fine-tuning (+33.29%)
  • mBERT also demonstrated strong improvement (+31.60%)
  • IndicBERTv1 showed moderate improvement (+20.53%)
  • XLM-RoBERTa had the smallest improvement (+19.84%)

Insight: MuRIL consistently outperformed, showing robustness across folds and the lowest standard deviation, indicating stable performance.

Discussion & Conclusion

Key Findings

MuRIL outperformed all models because it was pretrained on 17 Indian languages (including Hindi & Marathi) and included transliterated text, making it more effective for handling code-mixing and script variability.

The project tackled Hindi–Marathi code-mixed sentiment analysis in Roman script, an underexplored area in NLP. Our manually curated dataset from YouTube, Twitter (X), and online news articles covers natural intra-sentential, inter-sentential, and tag-switching cases.

Our pipeline achievement: Roman text preprocessing → Transliteration → Devanagari preprocessing enabled models to operate across scripts. Evaluation robustness: tested MuRIL, mBERT, IndicBERT, and XLM-R using K-Fold CV and cross-script testing.
